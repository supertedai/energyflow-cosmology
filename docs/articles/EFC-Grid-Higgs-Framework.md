## Abstract
The **Grid–Higgs Framework** extends the Energy-Flow Cosmology (EFC) by integrating Higgs field dynamics as the **structural nodes of spacetime**.  
In this view, gravity, dark matter, and black holes are not independent phenomena but emerge from the interplay between **entropy, energy flow, and grid stability**.  
Dark matter corresponds to *entropic tension* within the Higgs-anchored grid, while black holes mark collapse zones where energy flow halts under extreme entropy gradients.  
This formulation provides an empirically testable alternative to ΛCDM and emergent-gravity models through direct comparison with JWST, SDSS, Planck, and LIGO data.

---

## 1. Introduction
The ΛCDM model effectively fits large-scale data but fails to explain:
- The **Hubble tension**  
- The **dark matter problem**  
- **Black hole formation mechanisms**

The Grid–Higgs Framework proposes that these discrepancies arise from neglecting the **entropic structure of spacetime**.  
By incorporating Higgs bosons as **grid nodes**, the model unifies matter generation, gravity, and thermodynamics in one coherent system.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 The Grid Structure and Higgs Nodes
Spacetime is treated as a **dynamic entropic grid**, where Higgs bosons function as discrete, stabilizing nodes that regulate mass and energy distribution.

- Energy flow \( E_f \) is defined by:
  \[
  E_f = k_k \, ρ_H (1 - S)
  \]
  where \( ρ_H \) is the Higgs field density, \( k_k \) a coupling constant, and \( S \) normalized entropy.

- The Higgs field stabilizes the grid, localizing fluctuations and preventing spontaneous collapse.

---

### 2.2 Dark Matter as Entropic Tension
Instead of invoking new particles, dark matter is redefined as **entropic stress** within the Higgs field grid:

\[
ρ_{DM}^{eff} = λ ∇S
\]
where \( λ∇S \) represents entropic tension between Higgs nodes.

**Interpretation:**  
Gravitational anomalies arise from local energy retention within the grid — a direct result of entropy gradients rather than hidden mass.

---

### 2.3 Black Holes as Grid Collapse Points
Black holes occur when entropy gradients reach extreme levels:

\[
S \to 0 \Rightarrow E_f \to 0
\]
leading to structural collapse.  

Light speed becomes entropy-dependent:
\[
c(S) = c_0 (1 - αS)
\]
causing small observable deviations near event horizons, potentially testable by future interferometric missions.

---

### 2.4 Variable Gravitational Constant
Gravity emerges as an **entropic interaction**, with effective \( G(S) \) depending on entropy:
\[
G(S) = G_0 (1 - αS)
\]
suggesting weakened gravity in high-entropy (diffuse) regions and stronger coupling near low-entropy zones.

## 3. Empirical Validation

### 3.1 JWST and SDSS
- High-redshift JWST galaxies show **mass deficits** consistent with entropy-driven halos.  
- SDSS lensing data indicate stronger effects in **low-entropy clusters** and attenuation in **high-entropy voids** — matching the grid-tension predictions.

### 3.2 CMB Anisotropies
Predicted deviation of **7–10% at multipoles ℓ > 1000**, due to entropy damping:
\[
ΔT/T ≈ (∇S)^2
\]
aligning with Planck uncertainties and offering a measurable signature.

### 3.3 Gravitational Waves
LIGO data suggest stable wave speeds (\( Δc_g/c < 10^{-15} \)) in moderate-entropy regions, but the model predicts deviations in high-S domains — a falsifiable effect.
