EFC-D: Dynamics Model

Overview

EFC-D describes cosmic dynamics as the result of energy flow along entropy gradients. The central quantity is the energy field \(E_f(S)\), which varies with the local entropy density \(S\).

Energy–Entropy Relation

Dynamics arise when:

\[\nabla S \neq 0\]

Energy moves toward increasing entropy:

\[\vec{v}_E = -k \, \nabla S\]

with \(k\) controlling the field response.

Effective Potential

We define an effective potential:

\[\Phi_E(S) = -\int E_f(S) \, dS\]

Acceleration follows as:

\[\vec{a} = -\nabla \Phi_E .\]

Expansion Rate

The expansion rate predicted by EFC-D is:

\[H(S) = \sqrt{|E_f(S)|} \, (1 + S),\]

used for comparison with DESI, CMB, and BAO measurements.

EFC-S: Structure Model

Overview

EFC-S describes structure formation as the emergence of stable configurations in the entropy field. Halos, nodes, and filaments arise from local curvature in \(S(r)\).

Local Entropy Well

A halo is modeled as:

\[S(r) = S_0 \, e^{-r/r_s},\]

where \(r_s\) is the scale length.

Effective Density

The effective density follows directly from entropy curvature:

\[\rho_{\text{eff}}(r) = \nabla^2 S(r).\]

Rotation Curve Prediction

The predicted rotation velocity is:

\[v(r) = \sqrt{ r \, \partial_r \Phi_E(r) },\]

leading to dark-matter–free rotation curve fits for SPARC/MaNGA galaxies.

EFC-C\(_0\): Cognitive/Informational Layer

Overview

EFC-C\(_0\) describes how information and light interact with entropy through an effective refractive index. This layer connects EFC to observational lensing and signal propagation.

Entropy–Refractive Index Relation

We define:

\[n(S) = 1 + \alpha S,\]

where \(\alpha\) is the response coefficient.

Local Speed of Light

Light speed is reduced according to:

\[c(S) = \frac{c_0}{n(S)}.\]

Fermat Functional

Light paths minimize:

\[\tau = \int n(S) \, ds,\]

giving an entropy-driven lensing law.

Dual Lensing

Two effects appear:

  • geometric deviation

  • entropy-induced refraction

This forms the observational core of EFC lensing.

Light Dynamics: s0 → s1 Transitions

Overview

The s\(_0 \rightarrow\) s\(_1\) transition describes how light changes mode of propagation inside an entropy field.

Propagation Law

\[\frac{d\vec{k}}{ds} = -\nabla n(S),\]

which is optically equivalent to gravitational lensing, but driven by \(\nabla S\) rather than mass.

Coupling Between Layers

Light follows modified trajectories whenever:

\[\nabla S \neq 0.\]

This couples EFC-D (dynamics) and EFC-C\(_0\) (refraction) into a unified signal-propagation model.

Observables

Rotation Curves

Lensing Angle

\[\theta = \int \nabla n(S) \, ds.\]

Expansion Signal

EFC-D: Energy-Flow Dynamics

The local energy-flow potential \( E_f \) is defined as a function of mass density \( \rho \) and entropy \( S \):

\[ E_f = \rho (1 - S). \]

Interpretation:

  • high density and low entropy \(\Rightarrow\) large \(E_f\);
  • high entropy suppresses \(E_f\).

Density

The mass density is

\[ \rho = \frac{m}{V}. \]

Energy-Flow Rate

The temporal change in the energy-flow potential is written as

\[ \frac{dE_f}{dt} = \nabla_t E_f. \]

EFC-S: Entropy Field & Gradient

EFC starts from a thermodynamic field \( S(\mathbf{x}) \) representing the local entropy state of the matter–information configuration.

Entropy Field

The entropy field is modeled as

\[ S(\mathbf{x}) \in [0,1], \]

where

  • \( S = 0 \): highly ordered regions;
  • \( S = 1 \): high-entropy regions.

Entropy Gradient

The entropy gradient drives anisotropic flow:

\[ \nabla S(\mathbf{x}) = \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial S}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial S}{\partial z} \right). \]

Its magnitude is

\[ \lvert \nabla S \rvert = \sqrt{ \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial x} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial y} \right)^2 + \left( \frac{\partial S}{\partial z} \right)^2 }. \]

s0 → s1 Light Propagation Dynamics

Entropy Endpoints

We define two entropy endpoints:

  • \( s_0 \): low-entropy, high-structure endpoint;
  • \( s_1 \): high-entropy, high-diffusion endpoint.

The midpoint and entropy span are

\[ S_{\text{mid}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(s_0 + s_1), \qquad \Delta S = s_1 - s_0. \]

The normalized entropy coordinate is

\[ x(S) = \frac{S - S_{\text{mid}}}{\Delta S/2}. \]

Effective Speed of Light

The effective speed of light is modeled as

\[ c(S) = c_0 \left( 1 + a_{\text{edge}}\, x(S)^2 \right), \]

where \( a_{\text{edge}} \) controls the strength of edge-driven variation.

Light Travel Time

For a photon propagating along a path \( \gamma \), the observed travel time is

\[ t_{\text{obs}} = \int_{\gamma} \frac{dl}{c(S(l))}. \]

Lensing Behaviour

Low-entropy endpoints near \( s_0 \) act as focusing wells, while high-entropy regions near \( s_1 \) generate large-scale defocusing. This creates a dual-lens structure consistent with halo asymmetries.

All observable quantities are modulated by the entropy field \( S(\mathbf{x}) \), the energy-flow field \( E_f(\mathbf{x}) \), and the entropy-dependent light speed \( c(S) \).

Light Propagation

\[ t_{\text{obs}} = \int_{\gamma} \frac{dl}{c(S(l))}. \]

Lensing

\[ \text{Lensing} = \text{Mass Distribution} + S(\mathbf{x}) + c(S). \]

Rotation Curves

\[ v(r) = \sqrt{\, r \, \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial r} \, }. \]

Expansion Rate

The EFC expansion rate is

\[ H(E_f, S) = \sqrt{|E_f|}\,(1 + S). \]
