## Abstract
This hypothesis proposes that the **Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)**, observed at 2.725 K, represents a **thermodynamic gradient** sustained by quantum vacuum energy at the Planck scale.  
Building upon the **Energy–Entropy Theory** and **Grid–Higgs Framework**, it reinterprets the CMB as an *active equilibrium state* driven by continuous energy exchange, replacing the concept of a singular Big Bang with a **cyclical cosmological process**.  
The model quantifies vacuum–CMB energy differentials and predicts testable signatures in CMB anisotropies, gravitational waves, and large-scale structure formation.

---

## 1. Introduction
The standard cosmological model describes the CMB as residual radiation from the Big Bang.  
This hypothesis instead considers it a **steady-state thermodynamic equilibrium** maintained by energy flow between:
- the **Planck-scale quantum vacuum**, and  
- the **CMB energy density** at ~2.7 K.

The **Energy–Entropy Theory (Magnusson)** frames cosmic evolution as the interplay of energy flow and entropy gradients, rather than expansion from an initial singularity.

## 2. Fundamental Principles

- Cosmic dynamics are driven by thermodynamic gradients between the **quantum vacuum** and the **CMB**.  
- The CMB represents an **active equilibrium**, not a fossil afterglow.  
- Continuous energy exchange prevents universal thermodynamic stasis.

| CMB energy density | \( ρ_{CMB} \) | \( 7.56 × 10^{-14}\,J/m³ \) | Measured at 2.7 K |
| Pressure difference | \( ΔP \) | \( ρ_P - ρ_{CMB} \) | Drives cosmic energy flow |
| Observable volume | \( V \) | \( 3.57 × 10^{80}\,m³ \) | Hubble-scale volume |
| Gravitational scaling | \( γ \) | \( 1.67 × 10^{-129}\,kg^{-1}m^{-1}s \) | Derived from \( Gρ_{CMB}/c³ \) |
| Entropy differential | \( ΔS \) | \( 9.92 × 10^{66}\,J/K \) | Vacuum → CMB gradient |

Energy flow rate:
\[
\frac{dE}{dt} = -γ · V · \frac{ΔP}{ΔS}
\]

Calculated energy transfer:
\[
\frac{dE}{dt} ≈ 2.79 × 10^{35}\,J/s
\]
— matching the energy scale associated with observed cosmic expansion.
